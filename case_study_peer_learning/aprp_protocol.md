The Challenge
Business Problem: Safety-Critical Protocol Gaps in Vulnerable Populations
A provincial disability services agency provides state-funded supports (PDD ‚Äì Persons with Developmental Disabilities) to adults with developmental disabilities in residential care homes and community settings. These individuals‚Äîoften isolated from external oversight, dependent on paid staff for daily care, and historically taught to defer to authority‚Äîare at disproportionate risk of abuse, neglect, and exploitation.
Performance Gap Analysis:
Current State:

Staff inconsistently recognized subtle forms of abuse (emotional, exploitation, inappropriate use of restrictive procedures)
Unclear reporting pathways resulted in delayed or missed incident documentation
Power imbalances between staff and residents went unexamined, enabling exploitation
Staff lacked understanding of vulnerability factors specific to developmental disabilities
No systematic prevention framework‚Äîresponse was reactive rather than proactive

Desired State:

100% staff recognition and reporting of all suspected abuse incidents (6 categories: physical, sexual, emotional, exploitation, neglect, inappropriate restrictive procedures)
Timely, accurate reporting through established chain of command
Proactive abuse prevention through systemic changes (staff training, community inclusion, power-sharing with residents)
Increased staff awareness of vulnerability factors and personal responsibility in safeguarding
Complete incident documentation and follow-up review process

Root Cause: Instructional analysis confirmed that knowledge and awareness gaps were primary drivers. Staff needed to understand what constitutes abuse in a disability context (recognition challenge), understand the proper reporting mechanisms (procedural skill gap), and internalize responsibility for prevention (attitude/motivation gap).
Audience Challenges: Direct-Care Workers in High-Responsibility, Low-Autonomy Contexts
Audience Profile:

Demographics: Ages 22-65+, high school to diploma/certificate education, primarily female (70%+), diverse language backgrounds
Experience Range: 6 months to 20+ years in disability support
Motivation: Mixed‚Äîsome deeply committed advocates for disability rights; others viewing role as "just a job"
Constraints: Limited paid training time, high staff turnover (40%+ annually), competing demands (direct care + documentation + scheduling)
Attitude: May minimize incidents ("He always does that"), blame residents ("She makes it up"), or fear consequences of reporting ("Will I lose my job?")
Knowledge Gaps: Limited understanding of trauma responses, disability-specific communication differences, power dynamics in care relationships

Instructional Challenge:
This was not a simple knowledge-transfer problem. The training had to shift staff mindsets from viewing abuse as overt (stranger danger, visible injuries) to recognizing subtle, systemic forms of harm occurring within trusted relationships. Additionally, it needed to address psychological barriers to reporting (fear of retaliation, organizational loyalty, blame-shifting) while ensuring consistent protocol execution. The course had to balance compliance requirements with authentic behavior change‚Äîstaff needed to internalize that abuse prevention is their responsibility, not something that "happens to other organizations."

Process & Methodology
Systematic, Evidence-Based Design Approach
I applied the ADDIE framework integrated with Merrill's First Principles of Instruction, Bloom's Revised Taxonomy (targeting behavioral change at the "Apply" and "Analyze" levels), Kirkpatrick's Four Levels of Evaluation, and Social Cognitive Theory (addressing self-efficacy, outcome expectations, and environmental influences on behavior change) to ensure systematic, evidence-based design.
Needs Analysis (ADDIE Analysis Phase)
1. Performance Gap Analysis
Through stakeholder interviews with care home directors, frontline staff, and PDD program officers, I conducted task analysis to decompose critical staff responsibilities:

Recognize: Identify 6 abuse categories + indicators specific to PDD context (e.g., unexplained injuries, emotional withdrawal, sudden behavioral changes, sexual acting out, financial inconsistencies, compliance-based isolation)
Report: Follow chain of command (immediate supervisor ‚Üí home director ‚Üí PDD administrator ‚Üí FMS [Financial Management System] administrator for documentation)
Prevent: Apply vulnerability-reduction strategies (power-sharing with residents, community inclusion, staff training, monitoring)
Review: Participate in post-incident investigation and systemic improvement process

2. Audience & Context Analysis
Observation and interviews revealed:

Work Environment: High-stress, understaffed settings with competing demands (toileting, meals, medication, transportation, documentation)
Emotional Context: Staff often experienced secondary trauma, boundary confusion (residents as "family"), and organizational siloing (lack of information sharing)
Barrier to Reporting: Fear of job loss, blame culture ("Why didn't you prevent this?"), inadequate time for documentation, unclear escalation paths
Learning Preferences: Direct-care staff responded well to peer examples, real scenarios, and respect for their expertise (many had years of practical knowledge)

3. Root Cause Determination
Using Mager & Pipe's performance analysis framework, I confirmed that training was appropriate (supported by manager interviews noting knowledge gaps) but insufficient alone‚Äîsystemic changes (reporting culture, clear protocols, leadership accountability) were also required. However, training could address the awareness and skill foundation that enabled systemic change.
Design Framework Integration
Merrill's First Principles of Instruction:

Problem-centered: Module 1 opened with real scenarios of PDD residents in care settings (anonymized case studies from actual incidents in similar organizations)
Activation: "You've worked with residents who've experienced trauma. What signs have you noticed?"‚Äîconnected to staff experience
Demonstration: Video case study showing staff recognizing abuse early, following reporting protocol, and preventing escalation
Application: 4 branching scenarios with embedded decision points (resident interaction ‚Üí interpretation ‚Üí response ‚Üí feedback)
Integration: Staff reference card for point-of-care consultation, manager toolkit for ongoing support and cultural reinforcement

Bloom's Revised Taxonomy (Revised Edition‚ÄîAnderson & Krathwohl):
Designed learning objectives at higher-order levels:

Remember: Define 6 abuse categories (prerequisite knowledge)
Understand: Explain why PDD residents are vulnerable; interpret behavioral indicators as abuse warning signs
Apply: Recognize abuse in case scenarios; execute proper reporting protocol
Analyze: Identify root causes of vulnerability (power imbalances, isolation, learned compliance); evaluate systemic prevention strategies
Evaluate: Assess organizational practices for abuse-enabling conditions; judge when reporting escalation is necessary
Create: Design personal prevention strategies (boundary-setting, resident empowerment practices)

Assessment design focused on Apply and Analyze levels (not just recall), ensuring staff could transfer learning to complex, ambiguous situations.
Social Cognitive Theory (Bandura):
Addressed psychological barriers to behavior change:

Self-Efficacy: "Can I recognize abuse?" (scenario-based practice with feedback), "Can I report without being retaliated against?" (clear policy statements, testimonials from staff who reported)
Outcome Expectations: Staff believed reporting had positive impact‚Äîshowed data on how previous reports led to systemic improvements
Environmental Influences: Emphasized organizational commitment (director message), peer support (peer-led toolbox talks), and clear protocols (reference card at every workstation)

Assessment-First Design
I designed the 12-item assessment before developing content, ensuring learning objectives drove instruction (not decorative content). Items spanned three critical domains:
Domain 1: Recognition (40% of assessment)

Scenario interpretation: "Resident won't eat, cries when alone‚Äîabuse indicator or medical issue?" (requires analysis)
Indicator prioritization: "Which of these warrant urgent reporting?" (critical thinking)

Domain 2: Reporting & Response (35% of assessment)

Protocol sequence: "What's your first action?" (knowledge + application)
Barriers & bypass strategies: "Your supervisor is unavailable‚Äîwhat do you do?" (real-world problem-solving)
Critical items (flagged‚Äî100% accuracy required): "You can NEVER ___ without explicit permission" (safety gates)

Domain 3: Prevention & System Thinking (25% of assessment)

Root cause analysis: "Why are residents in isolated settings at higher risk?" (analytical thinking)
Prevention strategy evaluation: "Which practice best reduces power imbalances?" (judgment)

Passing Threshold: 80% overall, but any of 3 critical safety items (e.g., "stopping work when abuse is suspected," "reporting chain") resulted in automatic failure‚Äîreflecting that some competencies require mastery, not partial competence. Unlimited reassessment with targeted remediation.

Design Decisions & Solutions
Engagement Strategy: Bridging Compliance & Authentic Advocacy
Recognizing that direct-care staff had both compliance obligations AND genuine care for residents, I designed engagement around values alignment‚Äîpositioning abuse prevention not as a box-ticking requirement but as fundamental to their professional identity.
First 90 Seconds: Values-Based Hook
Module 1 opened with a testimonial video (real staff member, not actor):

"I've worked here 12 years. When I first started, I didn't recognize the signs. A resident who was really withdrawn, wouldn't make eye contact‚ÄîI just thought that was her personality. But then... [pause] I realized she was being emotionally abused by another staff member. I wish I'd known what to look for. Now I do. And I'm part of protecting her. That matters."

This framing accomplished several goals:

Established relevance without fear-mongering
Positioned reporting as professional pride (protecting residents you care for)
Normalized not knowing (reducing shame barrier)
Demonstrated that reporting had positive impact (countered fear of futility)

Progress & Autonomy:

Module menu with completion checkmarks (visible progress)
Confidence self-assessment ("How confident are you in recognizing abuse?") at start and end‚Äîshowing growth
Explicit permission for imperfect knowledge: "You don't have to be an expert. You just have to notice and report."

Friction Reduction:

Mobile-first design (works on personal phones‚Äîrespects staff's own time/device preference)
Auto-save (can pause mid-module)
No time limits (self-paced, respects interruptions during breaks)
Transcript/captions available (accessibility + asynchronous option)

Instructional Approach: Scenario-Driven, Consequence-Based Learning with Systemic Context
Branching Scenarios (Modules 2-4):
I designed 4 embedded branching scenarios where learners made decisions and witnessed consequences. Each scenario represented a different resident and abuse type, reflecting real care home complexity.
Scenario 1: Emotional Abuse (Module 2)
Setup: Staff member observes another caregiver frequently criticizing resident in front of others ("You're too slow," "You're so needy"). Resident becomes withdrawn, stops participating in activities.
Decision Point 1: Recognition

Choice A: "This is normal discipline for behavior management" ‚Üí INCORRECT: "Criticism in front of others causes shame. This is emotional abuse and violates dignity. Recognize it."
Choice B: "She's just having a bad day" ‚Üí PARTIALLY CORRECT: "It could be, but if it's ongoing, it becomes abuse. Pattern matters."
Choice C: "This is emotional abuse. It's causing harm." ‚Üí CORRECT: "Exactly. You've recognized the pattern. What do you do next?"

Decision Point 2: Response

Choice A: "Ignore it‚Äînot my resident, not my problem" ‚Üí INCORRECT: Consequence animation shows resident's withdrawal deepening, text: "Silence enables abuse. You have a duty of care to all residents."
Choice B: "Confront the other staff member" ‚Üí PARTIALLY CORRECT: "Your intention is good, but confrontation can escalate. Use the protocol."
Choice C: "Report to my supervisor immediately" ‚Üí CORRECT: "You've broken the cycle. Your supervisor can intervene and document."

Consequence Feedback:
Each choice triggered immediate, emotionally resonant feedback‚Äînot just "right/wrong" but narrative consequences showing how the choice affected the resident.
Scenario 2: Neglect (Module 2)
Setup: Understaffed shift, resident with mobility needs hasn't been toileted in hours, is sitting in soiled clothing. Staff are "doing their best with what we have."
Decision Points:

Recognition: Is this abuse or systemic failure? (Answer: Both‚Äîindividual staff neglect + organizational understaffing)
Response: Who do you report to? (Supervisor + document + escalate to management‚Äînot just individual staff member)
Prevention: What systemic changes would prevent this? (Staffing levels, toileting schedules, monitoring, accountability)

Consequence: Shows both immediate care for resident (dignity restored) and systemic outcome (staffing conversation with director about sustainable care models).
Scenario 3: Exploitation (Module 3)
Setup: Staff member realizes another staff member is using resident's government benefits to "pay for" personal favors (rides, snacks, free time). Resident doesn't understand the exchange isn't legitimate.
Decision Points:

Recognition: Is this abuse? (Yes‚Äîfinancial exploitation + power imbalance)
Reporting: This involves finances‚Äîdo I report or handle it myself? (ALWAYS report‚Äîfinancial crimes require specialized investigation)
Personal response: "But the resident benefits from the snacks..." (Irrelevant‚Äîconsent obtained through coercion isn't consent)

Scenario 4: Sexual Abuse/Inappropriate Restriction (Module 4)
Setup: Resident discloses that another staff member has been touching them inappropriately. Staff member fears being blamed ("Why didn't you report sooner?") or not being believed ("She makes things up").
Decision Points:

Immediate safety: Do you keep questioning the resident? (No‚Äîstop and listen; full investigation happens later)
Reporting: Do you investigate yourself? (No‚Äîimmediate supervisor + documented, then expert investigation)
Support: What do you tell the resident? ("Thank you for telling me. This is not your fault. We will keep you safe.")

Consequence: Shows the entire reporting chain, investigator engagement, and residential support plan‚Äînormalizing the process.
Why This Scenario Design Works:

Authenticity: Real situations staff encounter (not sanitized, unrealistic scenarios)
Emotional Encoding: Consequences are emotionally resonant‚Äîviewers remember the impact of choices
Complexity: Scenarios acknowledge real barriers (understaffing, systemic issues, fear of consequences) and show how to navigate them
Transfer: Staff can map real situations they encounter to scenario patterns, increasing application likelihood

Module 4: Prevent‚ÄîSystemic Approaches to Reducing Vulnerability (4 min)

Why Prevention Matters:

"If abuse can be prevented, it should be. That's part of your job."


4 Vulnerability-Reduction Strategies:

Authority & Power Imbalances: Shift from "caregiver telling resident what to do" to "supporting resident's choices"

Example: Instead of "It's medication time," ask "Are you ready for your medication?"
Rationale: Residents with input + choice are more resilient against abuse


Isolation & Community Inclusion: Connect residents to external relationships (family, community, advocates)

Example: Facilitate video calls with family, support community employment, encourage friendships
Rationale: People with external oversight are less vulnerable


Staff Training & Awareness: Ensure all staff understand abuse indicators + reporting protocol

Example: Toolbox talks, ongoing training, peer learning
Rationale: Trained staff catch abuse early


Monitoring & Accountability: Regular safety checks, resident feedback, staff supervision

Example: Resident surveys ("Do you feel safe?"), unannounced observations, supervision with feedback
Rationale: Abusers operate in secret; transparency reduces risk
